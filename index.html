The Course

Over the last decade, driven by advances in supercomputing power and the development of deep learning algorithms capable of learning from massive datasets, Artificial Intelligence (AI) has emerged as a transformative force in scientific discovery. AI has played a pivotal role in numerous breakthroughs, such as those recognized in the 2024 Nobel Prize. From tackling climate change to accelerating drug discovery and advancing particle physics, AI has revolutionized areas like automation, data analysis, predictive modeling, and simulations at unprecedented levels.


This course explores how cutting-edge AI technologies can be harnessed to drive scientific discovery and design. The course has two primary goals. First, it aims to equip students with foundational AI tools to tackle challenges in scientific research and innovation. To this end, the first half of the course introduces essential computational techniques, including statistical machine learning, deep learning, neural networks, and AI-based planning methods. Second, the course seeks to inspire students by showcasing real-world applications of AI in scientific discovery and design. Topics covered will include the use of AI in mathematical problem-solving, molecular dynamics, materials synthesis, life sciences, and geosciences.


Students will have opportunities to take deeper dives into foundational technologies or one or more science application domains via projects. The course format includes instructor-led lectures, guest presentations by leaders in AI and scientific fields, programming tutorials and team-based project presentations, offering students a hands-on and interdisciplinary learning experience.


Course Description from a Student's Perspective

This course offers a comprehensive introduction to the foundational algorithms and techniques in artificial intelligence, with a particular focus on scientific computing. It starts with the basics of AI, including statistical machine learning and various neural networks such as visual, sequence, and graph networks. What makes this course exciting is the deep dive into generative models and reinforcement learning. You’ll explore how these models work in complex scientific environments and learn how to apply them to solve real-world problems. The practical components are highly interactive. Using tools like Jupyter Notebook, you’ll get hands-on experience in implementing AI algorithms and applying them to scientific challenges. By the end of the course, you will not only understand data-driven and physics-driven algorithms but also be able to choose the right one depending on the scientific problem at hand, such as solving equations, molecular dynamics simulations, or designing materials. The group project also adds a collaborative element, helping you develop teamwork and project management skills. Overall, this course really prepares you to combine AI and scientific research, driving both innovation and real-world applications.

Course Policy

Late policy

Assignments are to be submitted by the due date listed. Each person will be allowed two days of extensions which can be applied to any combination of assignments (homework/projects only; exams excluded) during the semester without penalty. After that, a late penalty of 15% per day will be assigned. The use of a partial day will be counted as a full day. Use of extension days must be stated explicitly at the time of the late submission (by accompanying email to ALL TAs and the instructor), otherwise, late penalties will apply. Extensions cannot be used after the final day of classes (ie., December 8). Extension days cannot be rearranged after they are applied to a submission. Additional no-penalty late days may be introduced in the later part of the semester conditioned on the completion of the course evaluations (details to be finalized). Assignments, project reports, etc, will NOT BE accepted if they are more than five days late (and receive zero points). Additional extensions will be granted only due to serious and documented medical or family emergencies. Use the late days wisely!


Academic honesty

  • Unless stated otherwise, each student should write up their own solutions independently. You need to indicate the names of the people you discussed a problem with; ideally you should discuss with no more than two other people.
  • NO PART OF THE STUDENT'S ASSIGMENT (PROJECT, NOTES, ETC) SHOULD BE COPIED FROM ANOTHER STUDENT OR FROM OTHER RESEARCHERS OR FROM THE WEB (Plagiarism). We encourage you to interact amongst yourselves: you may discuss and obtain help with basic concepts covered in lectures or the textbook, homework specification (but not solution), and general ideas of program implementation (but not the code). However, unless otherwise noted, work turned in should reflect your own efforts and knowledge. Sharing or copying solutions is unacceptable and could result in failure of this course. We use copy detection software, so do not copy code and make changes (either from the Web or from other students). You are expected to take reasonable precautions to prevent others from using your work.
  • Any student not following these guidelines are subject to an automatic F (final grade).
